Parent Meeting Outline

60 minutes • Complete script for teachers

This outline was designed to be realistic.

Parents arrive late, phones ring, someone will want to speak more than time allows. The secret is maintaining structure without losing humanity. Use the suggested lines as inspiration — not as a fixed script.

0–10 MINUTES • OPENING

Welcome and Icebreaker

Stand to greet parents, thank them for attending, and start with a simple question:

→ "How was your child when they left for school today?"
→ Let 2 or 3 parents respond. This warms up the room and creates connection.

Present the agenda: "Today we're going to talk about bullying — not as someone else's problem, but as something we can all prevent together."

10–25 MINUTES • INFORMATION

What is and isn't bullying

Present the difference between occasional fighting and bullying (repetition + power imbalance). Use concrete examples without judgment.

→ Show the 3 profiles: victim, aggressor, and bystander — and explain that the bystander is the majority.
→ "Your child might be seeing bullying happen and not know what to do."
→ Distribute the Warning Signs Guide at this moment.
25–40 MINUTES • REFLECTION

Dynamics with parents

Choose a card from the Empathy Cards and read it aloud to the parents. Ask them to answer from their child's point of view.

→ Suggestion: "What would your child do if they saw a classmate being humiliated?"
→ Discuss in small groups (3–4 parents) for 5 minutes, then open to the whole room.
→ There is no right answer — the goal is to generate reflection, not judgment.
40–55 MINUTES • ACTION

What parents can do at home

Present the 3 concrete actions (next section). Distribute the at-home activities sheet.

→ "You don't need to become experts — you need to create space for the conversation to happen."
→ Distribute the Family Commitment Letter.
→ Explain: "I'll keep a copy. At the next meeting, we'll revisit what each family tried."
55–60 MINUTES • CLOSING

Closing and Thank You

Close with a statement that motivates, doesn't frighten:

→ "No school solves bullying alone. You are our most important partners."
→ Inform your communication channel for confidential reports.
→ Thank everyone for attending — especially parents attending for the first time.

Warning Signs Guide

To distribute to parents at the meeting — or send via school communication

For parents: Bullying is rarely reported directly by the child — they fear not being believed or the situation getting worse. Watch for these behavioral changes.

⚠️ Signs your child may be a victim

😰 Doesn't want to go to school

Makes vague excuses like stomach ache or headache — happens frequently

📱 Gets tense receiving messages

Suddenly looks away from phone or seems uncomfortable in your presence

🍽 Returns from school with damaged items

Comes home hungry or without money with no clear explanation

😔 Became withdrawn

Cries frequently, lost interest in activities they loved

👥 Stopped mentioning friends

Says no one to sit with at lunch or hasn't mentioned classmates

😴 Sleep difficulties

Frequent nightmares or wakes exhausted despite sleeping early

⚠️ Signs your child may be an aggressor

😤 Talks deprecatingly about classmates

Laughs when telling how someone "got in trouble" or was humiliated

💪 Needs to "win" in everything

Gets extremely frustrated when losing — even in games at home

💸 Appears with new money or objects

Can't explain where they came from

🙈 Can't put themselves in others' shoes

Reacts with indifference to others' suffering

If you identified any signs: Don't confront your child with accusations. Create a calm moment and say: "I've noticed you haven't been doing well. You don't have to tell me everything now, but know that I'm here." Then contact the school.

3 At-Home Activities

For parents to talk with their children after the meeting

Bullying stops at school when it starts at home
These activities take less than 20 minutes each and require no special materials.
ACTIVITY 1 • 15 MINUTES • ANY DAY

The Best Part and Hardest Part Game

At dinner, everyone at the table (including parents!) shares: 1 good thing that happened today and 1 difficult thing. Without judgment, without immediate advice — just listening.

Why it works: Creates a habit of daily conversation, and when something serious happens, your child will already know they can tell you.

"I'll start: the best part of my day was... and the hardest part was..."
ACTIVITY 2 • 10 MINUTES • WEEKEND

The Forbidden Question

Ask your child: "Is there anyone in your class that almost no one talks to?" and then: "Why do you think that happens?"

It's not to solve — it's for you to understand what's happening through their eyes.

"There's no right answer. I just want to understand what your class is like."
ACTIVITY 3 • 20 MINUTES • ONCE A MONTH

The Relationship Check-in

Ask your child to draw or write the names of 3 people they feel good with at school. Talk about why those people are important to them.

If they can't name anyone, that's an important signal — talk to the school.

"Who are the people who make you smile at school?"
Phrase to print and put on the refrigerator:

"Children who feel heard at home are more likely to ask for help when they need it."
