EMPATHY DYNAMICS

30 Complete Activities for Anti-Bullying and Emotional Development

Ages 4th-9th Grade | Complete Educational Program

The Other's Shoe

01
TIME
20 min
AGE GROUP
4th–9th grade
GROUP
Whole class
OBJECTIVE
Develop the ability to see situations from another person's perspective.
HOW TO DO IT
1
Ask each student to remove one shoe and place it in the center of the circle.
2
Each student picks up another classmate's shoe and holds or tries it on.
3
Ask: "What would it be like to walk one day in this shoe? What kind of person wears it?"
4
Each student briefly shares what they imagined.
FINAL REFLECTION
"What did this activity teach you about judging others before you know them?"

Letter to Myself

02
TIME
25 min
AGE GROUP
5th–9th grade
GROUP
Individual
OBJECTIVE
Strengthen self-esteem and self-compassion after difficult situations.
HOW TO DO IT
1
Distribute paper and ask each student to write a letter to themselves.
2
Theme: "What would I say to myself on the day I felt worthless?"
3
Those who wish to share can do so — it's never mandatory.
4
Keep the letters and deliver them at the end of the school year.
FINAL REFLECTION
"What would you say to someone who is going through what you have already experienced?"

The Web of Empathy

03
TIME
15 min
AGE GROUP
4th–9th grade
GROUP
Whole class
OBJECTIVE
Show how the actions of some affect everyone — and how empathy connects people.
HOW TO DO IT
1
Seat the class in a circle. One student holds a ball of yarn.
2
He says something positive about a classmate and tosses the yarn to them (holding the thread).
3
Continue until everyone is connected by the web.
4
Ask one student to let go of the thread — show how the entire web is affected.
FINAL REFLECTION
"What happens to the class when someone is excluded or treated badly?"

Traffic Light of Emotions

04
TIME
20 min
AGE GROUP
4th–7th grade
GROUP
Whole class
OBJECTIVE
Teach emotional self-regulation using the traffic light symbol.
HOW TO DO IT
1
Explain: Red = stop and breathe. Yellow = think before acting. Green = act calmly.
2
Read conflict situations and ask students to identify which "light" they're at.
3
In pairs, practice what they would do at each light for the same situation.
4
Share the strategies with the class.
FINAL REFLECTION
"Which light do you spend the most time at when someone provokes you? What helps you move forward?"

The Balloon of Words

05
TIME
20 min
AGE GROUP
4th–8th grade
GROUP
Whole class
OBJECTIVE
Demonstrate the weight of words — how they can build or destroy.
HOW TO DO IT
1
Inflate a balloon for each student. Each balloon represents a person.
2
Read offensive words (common insults). With each one, squeeze the balloon hard.
3
Inflate the balloon again — but the marks remain.
4
Show that kind words don't erase the damage from insults.
FINAL REFLECTION
"What marks do you carry from words you've heard? And what words would you never want to hear again?"

The Positive Note

06
TIME
15 min
AGE GROUP
4th–9th grade
GROUP
Individual
OBJECTIVE
Cultivate the habit of recognizing and valuing classmates' qualities.
HOW TO DO IT
1
Each student writes their name on a piece of paper and folds it.
2
Papers are drawn — each student receives another classmate's name.
3
Write an anonymous note with something genuine you admire in that person.
4
Deliver the notes and observe the class's reactions.
FINAL REFLECTION
"How did you feel being recognized by someone who maybe didn't realize they were watching you?"

The Role Play Game

07
TIME
30 min
AGE GROUP
6th–9th grade
GROUP
Groups of 3
OBJECTIVE
Understand the three roles in bullying: victim, aggressor, and witness.
HOW TO DO IT
1
Divide into groups of 3. Draw roles: victim, aggressor, witness.
2
Each group acts out a conflict situation for 3 minutes.
3
Repeat by swapping roles among members.
4
Discuss in full class: which role was hardest to play?
FINAL REFLECTION
"What does the witness role have to do with what actually happens in your classroom?"

The Qualities Clothesline

08
TIME
25 min
AGE GROUP
4th–9th grade
GROUP
Individual
OBJECTIVE
Promote mutual recognition and strengthen collective self-esteem.
HOW TO DO IT
1
Hang a clothesline with clothespins in the classroom. Each pin has a student's name.
2
Distribute colored paper strips. Each student writes a quality of each classmate.
3
Attach the strips to their respective classmates' clothespins.
4
Each student reads their qualities aloud — or silently, if they prefer.
FINAL REFLECTION
"Was there a quality that surprised you? Did someone see you in a way you never noticed in yourself?"

The Class Agreement

09
TIME
30 min
AGE GROUP
4th–9th grade
GROUP
Whole class
OBJECTIVE
Collectively build rules of coexistence that make sense for students.
HOW TO DO IT
1
Ask: "What do you need to feel safe and respected in this classroom?"
2
Each student writes 3 rules on post-its and sticks them on the board.
3
Group similar ideas and vote on the 5 most important.
4
Create a poster with the "Class Agreement" signed by all.
FINAL REFLECTION
"Why is it different to follow a rule that you helped create?"

Map of Emotions

10
TIME
20 min
AGE GROUP
4th–7th grade
GROUP
Individual
OBJECTIVE
Expand emotional vocabulary and awareness of your own feelings.
HOW TO DO IT
1
Distribute a human silhouette drawn on paper for each student.
2
Ask them to color the body regions where they "feel" each emotion (anger in chest, fear in belly...).
3
Write the names of the corresponding emotions.
4
Compare the maps in groups and identify similarities.
FINAL REFLECTION
"Where in your body do you feel it when someone hurts you? And when someone respects you?"

Kindness Mission

11
TIME
One week
AGE GROUP
4th–9th grade
GROUP
Individual
OBJECTIVE
Turn empathy into concrete action in daily school life.
HOW TO DO IT
1
Each student secretly draws a classmate — their "kindness target" for the week.
2
For 5 days, practice at least one kind act per day with that classmate.
3
On Friday, reveal who was your "angel" and share what you did.
4
Discuss: Was it difficult? What difference did it make to the class climate?
FINAL REFLECTION
"What happens to the class when kindness becomes a habit and not an exception?"

Theater of Everyday Life

12
TIME
35 min
AGE GROUP
5th–9th grade
GROUP
Groups of 4
OBJECTIVE
Practice assertive responses to real situations of bullying and exclusion.
HOW TO DO IT
1
Distribute cards with everyday situations (teasing, exclusion, nickname-calling).
2
Each group acts out the situation the "wrong" way — as it usually happens.
3
The class shouts "Cut!" and suggests a different ending.
4
The group re-enacts with the suggested ending.
FINAL REFLECTION
"Why is it easier to know what to do in a play than when it's real?"

The Human Knot

13
TIME
15 min
AGE GROUP
4th–9th grade
GROUP
Whole class
OBJECTIVE
Work cooperation, communication, and problem-solving in a group.
HOW TO DO IT
1
The class stands in a circle with everyone extending their hands to the center.
2
Each hand holds a different classmate's hand — forming a "human knot".
3
Without letting go of hands, the group must untangle until forming a circle.
4
Discuss the strategies used and moments of frustration.
FINAL REFLECTION
"What does this activity have to do with resolving conflicts in the classroom?"

The Empty Chair

14
TIME
20 min
AGE GROUP
6th–9th grade
GROUP
Whole class
OBJECTIVE
Reflect on the impact of exclusion and silence in the face of others' suffering.
HOW TO DO IT
1
Place an empty chair in the center of the circle, saying it represents an excluded student.
2
Ask each student to speak to the chair: what they wished they had said or done for that person.
3
Reflect in silence for 1 minute.
4
Discuss who might be "in that chair" in your classroom today.
FINAL REFLECTION
"Is there someone in our class who might be feeling like that right now? What can we do?"

15
TIME
30 min
AGE GROUP
4th–9th grade
GROUP
Individual
OBJECTIVE
Celebrate diversity as wealth and not as a reason for exclusion.
HOW TO DO IT
1
Each student draws or writes on a card: one unique thing that only they have.
2
The cards form a collective mural on the classroom wall.
3
The class takes a "guided tour" of the mural — each student presents theirs.
4

Fishing for Situations

16
TIME
25 min
AGE GROUP
4th–8th grade
GROUP
Groups of 4
OBJECTIVE
Develop ethical reasoning in the face of moral dilemmas in school.
HOW TO DO IT
1
Write dilemma situations on cards and place them in a box (the "lake").
2
Groups "fish" a situation and have 5 minutes to decide what they would do.
3
Present the decision and reasoning to the class.
4
The class debates: do you agree? Would you do something different?
FINAL REFLECTION
"What's harder: knowing what's right or having the courage to do what's right?"

Classmate Mirror

17
TIME
15 min
AGE GROUP
4th–7th grade
GROUP
Pairs
OBJECTIVE
Practice full attention to another person and strengthen empathetic observation.
HOW TO DO IT
1
In pairs: one student makes slow movements — the other is the "mirror".
2
Without speaking, the mirror reproduces each gesture with complete attention.
3
After 3 minutes, swap roles.
4
Conversation: What was it like being completely focused on another person?
FINAL REFLECTION
"When was the last time you gave total attention to someone without thinking about anything else?"

Chain of Empathy

18
TIME
20 min
AGE GROUP
4th–9th grade
GROUP
Whole class
OBJECTIVE
Show how an act of empathy can spread and transform the environment.
HOW TO DO IT
1
The first student makes a kind gesture to the classmate next to them (compliment, help, smile).
2
That classmate replicates it for the next person — and so on until the circle is complete.
3
Record on the board each gesture practiced.
4
Count: how many gestures did the whole class receive in just a few minutes?
FINAL REFLECTION
"If each of you did this with one person outside this room today, how many people would be affected?"

19

30 min

6th–9th grade
GROUP
Individual
OBJECTIVE

HOW TO DO IT
1
Distribute sheets with a horizontal line — this is the "life line".
2
Each student marks moments of high and low emotional peaks over school time.
3
Identify the events that caused each peak — both positive and negative.
4
Those who wish to share voluntarily with the class can do so.

HOW TO DO IT
1
Draw a thermometer on the board with a scale from 0 to 10.

Building Bridges

23
TIME
30 min
AGE GROUP
5th–9th grade
GROUP
Pairs
OBJECTIVE
Practice conflict resolution through structured dialogue.
HOW TO DO IT
1
Form pairs with students who had recent friction (or use fictional situations).
2
Each one speaks for 2 minutes without interruption: "How I felt and what I needed."
3
The other repeats what they heard — without judgment — to confirm understanding.
4
Together, write an agreement for coexistence between the two.
FINAL REFLECTION
"What was harder: saying what you felt or listening to the other side without defending yourself?"

Circle of Trust

24
TIME
25 min
AGE GROUP
6th–9th grade
GROUP
Whole class
OBJECTIVE
Build a safe space where students can be vulnerable without fear of judgment.
HOW TO DO IT
1
Establish the rule: what is said in the circle stays in the circle.
2
The teacher starts by sharing something personal and genuine — setting the example.
3
Pass an object (stone, ball) — whoever holds it can speak. Those who don't want to, pass.
4
No one comments or judges during the sharing.
FINAL REFLECTION
"How does it feel knowing you can be yourself here without being judged?"

Active Listening

28
TIME
20 min
AGE GROUP
5th–9th grade
GROUP
Pairs
OBJECTIVE
Practice genuine listening — without judgment, interruption, or immediate advice.
HOW TO DO IT
1
In pairs: one speaks for 3 minutes about something that bothers them in school.
2
The other listens in absolute silence — no speaking, no expressions.
3
Then the listener repeats in their own words what they understood.
4
The speaker evaluates: "Did you truly understand me?"
FINAL REFLECTION
"What's the difference between listening to respond and listening to understand?"

The Phrase That Stuck

30
TIME
25 min
AGE GROUP
5th–9th grade
GROUP
Whole class
OBJECTIVE
Reflect on the lasting impact of words — both those that build and those that destroy.
HOW TO DO IT
1
Each student thinks of a phrase someone said to them that they never forgot.
2
Write the phrase on a paper — without identifying who said it.
3
The papers are shuffled and read aloud by the teacher.
4
The class identifies: did this phrase build or destroy? What does it say about the person who said it?
FINAL REFLECTION
"What phrase do you want your classmates to remember about you in 10 years?"
